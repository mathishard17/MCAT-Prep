## CARS-P01
- **Discipline:** CARS
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage developing ideas from John Stuart Mill, *On Liberty* (1859), public domain (Project Gutenberg eBook #34901, https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/34901). No text is reproduced verbatim.
- **Passage:** A society that prizes its own improvement must decide how much latitude to grant the individual whose convictions diverge from the common view. One influential answer holds that power may rightfully be used against a competent adult's will for a single reason: to prevent that person from harming others. A person's own good, whether physical or moral, is not by itself a sufficient warrant for compulsion. Where only his own interests are at stake, each person is the proper authority over his own body and mind; his neighbors may reason with him, persuade him, or plead with him, but they may not coerce him so long as his conduct concerns himself alone.

  This principle is easier to state than to honor, because the pressure that most threatens individuality is rarely the pressure of law. More often it is the quieter tyranny of opinion and custom—the disposition of a majority to impose its own tastes and codes of conduct as rules binding on everyone. Such social tyranny can be more formidable than many kinds of political oppression, for it reaches more deeply into the details of daily life and leaves fewer avenues of escape. A citizen may be entirely free of the magistrate and yet wholly captive to the expectations of his community.

  The argument for liberty is most compelling in the case of thought and its expression. Suppose an opinion is silenced. If the opinion happens to be correct, those who suppress it deprive humanity of the chance to exchange error for truth. If it is mistaken, they lose something scarcely less valuable: the sharper perception and livelier impression of truth that is produced by its collision with error. Even a true belief, held merely as an inherited prejudice and never tested against objection, tends to decay into a lifeless formula rather than a living conviction. Its meaning is forgotten, its grounds are neglected, and it survives as a set of words that accomplishes little. Truth keeps its vitality only when it is continually contested.

  It follows that we cannot justify silencing dissent simply by appealing to our confidence in our own correctness. To treat an opinion as certain because we have never heard it refuted is to confuse two very different things: the assurance that comes from having invited and answered every objection, and the mere absence of doubt. To silence discussion is, in effect, to assume one's own infallibility, and history offers ample evidence that received opinions have often been wrong. The ages that persecuted their heretics were as certain of themselves as we are of our own settled beliefs.

  None of this implies that liberty is boundless. The same reasoning that protects the free circulation of ideas also permits restraint when expression becomes a direct incitement to injury. The line is drawn not at the offense an opinion gives, but at the harm an action causes. To be secure, liberty must rest on a principle its defenders will uphold even when the opinion in question strikes them as false, offensive, or absurd—for a freedom extended only to congenial views is no freedom at all.

### CARS-P01-Q1
- **Stem:** Which statement best expresses the central thesis of the passage?
- **A:** Governments should regulate private conduct whenever it risks moral harm to the individual.
- **B:** Individual liberty, especially freedom of thought and expression, should be protected and limited only by the prevention of harm to others.
- **C:** The opinions of a well-informed majority are the most reliable guide to social progress.
- **D:** Free expression is valuable only when the opinions expressed turn out to be true.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage repeatedly grounds legitimate restraint in harm to *others* and treats liberty of thought as the strongest case for freedom. (A) inverts the author's rejection of coercion for a person's "own good." (C) contradicts the warning about the "tyranny of the majority." (D) is refuted by the argument that even false opinions have value through "collision with error."

### CARS-P01-Q2
- **Stem:** According to the passage, why does silencing even a *false* opinion cause a loss?
- **A:** False opinions frequently turn out to be true after further investigation.
- **B:** Suppressing any opinion violates a legal right to speech.
- **C:** Contact with error sharpens and enlivens the perception of the truth that opposes it.
- **D:** False opinions are needed to fill gaps in incomplete theories.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** The passage states that even a wrong opinion yields "the sharper perception and livelier impression of truth that is produced by its collision with error." (A) describes the case of a *true* silenced opinion, not a false one. (B) concerns legality, which the passage does not make the basis of this particular point. (D) is not claimed.

### CARS-P01-Q3
- **Stem:** The passage suggests that a true belief held only as an untested prejudice will most likely:
- **A:** strengthen over time as it is repeated.
- **B:** lose its living force and survive as an empty formula.
- **C:** become a direct incitement to harm.
- **D:** convert its holder into a defender of free discussion.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage says such a belief "tends to decay into a lifeless formula rather than a living conviction," its grounds neglected. (A) contradicts the claim that vitality requires contestation. (C) confuses the separate discussion of expression that incites injury. (D) is not implied.

### CARS-P01-Q4
- **Stem:** Which claim about "social tyranny" is best supported by the passage?
- **A:** It is less dangerous than political oppression because it lacks the force of law.
- **B:** It operates only through formal institutions such as courts.
- **C:** It can be more oppressive than legal coercion because it pervades everyday life and is harder to escape.
- **D:** It disappears once a society guarantees legal freedoms.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** The passage argues social tyranny "can be more formidable than many kinds of political oppression" because it "reaches more deeply into the details of daily life." (A) and (D) are the opposite of the author's point (a citizen "free of the magistrate" may still be "captive"). (B) misidentifies its mechanism as formal/legal.

### CARS-P01-Q5
- **Stem:** A public official argues, "We may ban this pamphlet because its claims are plainly mistaken and no reasonable person doubts our judgment." The author would most likely respond that this reasoning:
- **A:** is sound, since obviously mistaken claims deserve no protection.
- **B:** improperly assumes the official's own infallibility and mistakes absence of doubt for genuine certainty.
- **C:** is acceptable only if the pamphlet offends community standards.
- **D:** is correct because the majority's confidence guarantees the claim is false.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The author holds that "to silence discussion is, in effect, to assume one's own infallibility" and distinguishes tested certainty from "the mere absence of doubt." (A) and (D) endorse exactly what the author rejects. (C) invokes offense, which the author explicitly says is *not* where the line should be drawn (harm from action is).

## CARS-P02
- **Discipline:** CARS
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage developing ideas from W. E. B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903), public domain (Project Gutenberg eBook #408, https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/408). No text is reproduced verbatim; the author's coined terms ("double consciousness," "the veil," "second sight," "the color line") are discussed as concepts.
- **Passage:** To grow up as a member of a group that others view through a screen of contempt or pity is to acquire an unusual angle of vision. One classic account of American life describes the experience of being treated as a problem—of sensing, from early childhood, that the surrounding society has already fixed a question mark upon one's very existence. The person so situated learns to see himself twice: once through his own eyes, and once through the eyes of a world that measures him by a standard it does not apply to itself.

  This author names the resulting condition a *double consciousness*. It is, in his account, the sense of forever appraising one's own worth by the yardstick of a society that looks on with a mixture of scorn and detachment. The self that emerges is not unified but doubled: an American and, at the same time, a member of a people set apart—two souls, two ways of thinking, two sets of aspirations that refuse to be reconciled, lodged within a single body, whose warring ideals threaten to pull it apart. The sheer effort required merely to keep this divided self from disintegrating is itself a hidden tax levied on those who must pay it.

  The metaphor the author uses for the barrier between the two worlds is the *veil*. Those born within it can see out; the wider world, glancing in, perceives only a dim figure and rarely troubles to look more closely. The veil is not merely a wall of law or custom but a difference in perception, each side reading the other through inherited images rather than direct acquaintance. Yet the author insists that the person behind the veil is granted a kind of *second sight*—an ability to perceive contradictions in a society that the comfortable majority can afford to ignore.

  Crucially, the author refuses the two easy resolutions of the divided self. He does not wish to bleach his distinct identity into a colorless sameness, surrendering everything particular to his heritage; nor does he wish to retreat into separateness and refuse the wider culture its genuine goods. What he desires is to be both, without penalty—to fuse the doubled self into a truer self in which neither of the older selves is lost. He would make it possible to be at once a full participant in the common life and the inheritor of a particular tradition, without having the door of opportunity shut roughly in his face.

  The larger diagnosis is compressed into a single forecast: that the central problem of the coming century would be the problem of the color line—the drawing of a boundary that distributes rights and recognition according to visible difference. The claim is not merely that prejudice causes suffering, though it plainly does. It is that a society organized around such a line distorts the vision of everyone within it, dividing not only people from people but the self from itself.

### CARS-P02-Q1
- **Stem:** As used in the passage, "double consciousness" most nearly refers to:
- **A:** the ability to hold two contradictory factual beliefs at the same time.
- **B:** an awareness of oneself formed both from one's own perspective and from the devaluing perspective of the surrounding society.
- **C:** a deliberate strategy of concealing one's opinions from authorities.
- **D:** the tendency of a majority to ignore minority viewpoints.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage defines it as seeing oneself "once through his own eyes, and once through the eyes of a world that measures him" by an external standard. (A) describes ordinary cognitive dissonance, not the concept. (C) is unrelated to the self-perception theme. (D) describes the majority's behavior, not the doubled self-awareness of the person behind the veil.

### CARS-P02-Q2
- **Stem:** The author's use of the "veil" primarily emphasizes that:
- **A:** legal barriers are the only meaningful obstacle between groups.
- **B:** the separation between the two worlds is partly a difference in perception, with the majority failing to look closely.
- **C:** those inside the veil cannot perceive the outside world at all.
- **D:** the barrier is easily removed once laws change.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage calls the veil "not merely a wall of law or custom but a difference in perception," and says the outside world "perceives only a dim figure and rarely troubles to look more closely." (A) is contradicted ("not merely a wall of law"). (C) is wrong—those within "can see out." (D) is not claimed and conflicts with the depth attributed to the barrier.

### CARS-P02-Q3
- **Stem:** Which best captures the resolution of the divided self that the author endorses?
- **A:** Complete assimilation that erases his particular heritage.
- **B:** Withdrawal into a separate community that rejects the wider culture.
- **C:** A merging of both identities into a truer self, losing neither, with full access to opportunity.
- **D:** Acceptance of the color line as a permanent feature of society.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** The author "refuses the two easy resolutions" and seeks "to be both, without penalty—to fuse the doubled self into a truer self in which neither of the older selves is lost." (A) and (B) are precisely the options he rejects. (D) contradicts his diagnosis of the color line as a distortion to be overcome.

### CARS-P02-Q4
- **Stem:** The passage presents "second sight" as:
- **A:** a supernatural power unrelated to social position.
- **B:** an insight into the society's contradictions available to those positioned behind the veil.
- **C:** a disadvantage that prevents clear judgment.
- **D:** the majority's ability to understand the minority.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The author says the person behind the veil is granted "a kind of second sight—an ability to perceive contradictions in a society that the comfortable majority can afford to ignore." (A) misreads the figurative term as literal. (C) inverts it into a deficit. (D) assigns the insight to the wrong group.

### CARS-P02-Q5

- **A:** affects only the group placed on the disadvantaged side of the line.

## CARS-P03
- **Discipline:** CARS
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage developing ideas from Mary Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792), public domain (Project Gutenberg eBook #3420, https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/3420). No text is reproduced verbatim.
- **Passage:** An argument advanced near the close of the eighteenth century holds that the apparent inferiority of women is not a fact of nature but a product of their upbringing. If women often appear frivolous, vain, or incapable of sustained reasoning, this author contends, it is because they have been deliberately cultivated to be so—taught from infancy that their power lies in charm and beauty rather than in understanding.

  The foundation of the argument is a claim about what makes any being worthy of respect. The distinguishing excellence of a human being, the author maintains, is reason; it is through the exercise of reason that virtue is acquired and knowledge attained. Whatever confers dignity on a man on this account must confer equal dignity on a woman, for the faculty of reason is not divided by sex. To praise in women a cluster of qualities—softness, docility, a pleasing exterior—that are unconnected with, or even opposed to, the growth of reason is therefore to flatter them at the expense of their humanity. It is to value them as one values an ornament, prized for the pleasure it gives another rather than for any perfection of its own.

  The author is especially severe toward those who counsel that a woman's education should be organized entirely around the project of pleasing men. Such advice, however gallantly phrased, treats women as instruments of others' comfort. It manufactures exactly the weaknesses it later points to as proof of natural incapacity: a mind fed only on romances and manners, and denied serious study, will indeed grow shallow. The remedy is not gentler flattery but genuine instruction. Grant women the same rational cultivation offered to men, the author argues, and their supposed defects will largely dissolve.

  The argument turns pointedly on the concept of virtue. If virtue is a single thing, grounded in reason, then it cannot have one standard for men and another for women. A morality that demands of women only obedience and modesty, while reserving for men the fuller virtues of courage, justice, and independent judgment, is not truly a morality at all; it is a code of manners disguised as ethics. Worse, it is self-defeating even by its own domestic aims: a mother who has never been permitted to reason cannot teach her children to reason, and a companion kept in perpetual childhood cannot be the rational partner that a good marriage requires.

  The author anticipates the objection that such claims threaten the social order, and replies that the reverse is true. A society is strengthened, not weakened, when half its members are educated to think. The demand is not for privilege but for the removal of an artificial handicap—for the opportunity to develop faculties that all human beings share. To withhold that opportunity and then to cite the resulting weakness as evidence of unfitness is to argue in a circle, mistaking the effects of a poor education for the verdict of nature.

### CARS-P03-Q1

- **B:** the result of an upbringing that cultivates charm rather than reason.

- **D:** evidence that reason differs between the sexes.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage opens by asserting the "inferiority of women is not a fact of nature but a product of their upbringing," and closes by calling the weakness an "artificial handicap." (A) and (D) are exactly the positions the author refutes. (C) misattributes the cause to women's choices rather than their education.

- **D:** Because reason guarantees obedience to social authority.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage states reason is "the distinguishing excellence of a human being," the means by which "virtue is acquired and knowledge attained," and that it "is not divided by sex." ### CARS-P03-Q4

### CARS-P03-Q5

- **A:** treats a cause and its effect as independent confirmations of each other.

## CARS-P04
- **Discipline:** CARS
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage developing ideas from Thorstein Veblen, *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899), public domain (Project Gutenberg eBook #833, https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/833). No text is reproduced verbatim; Veblen's coined terms ("conspicuous leisure," "conspicuous consumption," "invidious comparison") are discussed as concepts.
- **Passage:** Why do people so often spend money in ways calculated less to satisfy their wants than to be noticed? A classic analysis of economic life argues that much of what we consume is governed not by usefulness but by the desire to display status. In communities where wealth has become the accepted basis of esteem, the possession of goods is never merely private; it is a message addressed to others.

  The analysis begins from the observation that, in such societies, esteem attaches to the ability to afford waste. In an earlier stage, the mark of high standing was *conspicuous leisure*—visible abstention from productive work, which advertised that one was wealthy enough not to labor. The gentleman demonstrated his rank precisely by doing nothing useful, and by maintaining others whose idleness he could afford to sponsor. As populations grew and neighbors became strangers, however, leisure lost some of its communicative power: one cannot display one's idleness to people who never observe how one spends the day. The burden of demonstration therefore shifted from leisure to consumption, because goods, unlike hours, can be exhibited to strangers at a glance.

  Hence what the author calls *conspicuous consumption*: expenditure whose real purpose is to exhibit the payer's capacity to spend. The value of many prized articles, on this view, lies substantially in their very costliness. An expensive item testifies to the means of its owner; were it cheap, it would testify to nothing, however useful it might be. This is why marks of evident expense are so readily mistaken for marks of beauty, and why an object revealed to be inexpensive can suddenly appear shabby though its physical qualities are unchanged. We have learned to read price as a sign of worth.

  Underlying both leisure and consumption is a mechanism the author calls *invidious comparison*—the restless measuring of oneself against others. Because status is relative, no absolute level of wealth is ever sufficient; each gain merely resets the standard against which the next comparison is made. The result is a competition that cannot end, since its aim is not any fixed quantity of goods but a position ahead of one's neighbors. Emulation therefore drives spending steadily upward, as those below strive to match those above and those above strive to preserve their distance.

  The account extends even into the household. A person too busy earning to display leisure may delegate the display to others—to a spouse whose refinement and freedom from useful work advertise the earner's success, or to servants whose very presence signals means. The consumption and the leisure are then vicarious, performed by one person to the credit of another.

  The argument is deliberately unsettling, for it suggests that a great deal of ordinary economic behavior serves an honorific rather than a practical function. What appears to be the free pursuit of comfort turns out, on inspection, to be shaped by an audience—by the silent, constant estimation of how one appears in the eyes of others whose good opinion is measured in the currency of visible expense.

### CARS-P04-Q1
- **Stem:** The passage's central argument is that a great deal of consumption is best explained by:
- **A:** the practical usefulness of the goods people buy.
- **B:** the desire to display wealth and status to others.
- **C:** the natural human need for comfort and security.
- **D:** the declining cost of manufactured goods.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage states consumption is "governed not by usefulness but by the desire to display status" and is "a message addressed to others." (A) and (C) name the practical motives the author sets aside. (D) is never offered as an explanation.

### CARS-P04-Q2
- **Stem:** According to the passage, why did consumption largely replace leisure as the primary display of status?
- **A:** Leisure became more expensive than goods.
- **B:** Laws restricted the display of idleness.
- **C:** As neighbors became strangers, goods could be shown to onlookers while idleness could not.
- **D:** People came to value work more than idleness.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** The passage explains that "leisure lost some of its communicative power" among strangers because "one cannot display one's idleness to people who never observe how one spends the day," whereas "goods... can be exhibited to strangers at a glance." (A), (B), and (D) are not stated and misrepresent the visibility-based reasoning.

### CARS-P04-Q3
- **Stem:** The claim that an item "revealed to be inexpensive can suddenly appear shabby though its physical qualities are unchanged" primarily illustrates that:
- **A:** cheaper goods are usually of lower physical quality.
- **B:** perceived worth is tied to cost rather than to the object's intrinsic properties.
- **C:** beauty and expense are entirely independent of each other.
- **D:** consumers cannot judge the usefulness of goods.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The example shows that judgments of worth track price, not the object's unchanged physical qualities—"we have learned to read price as a sign of worth." (A) contradicts the point (the qualities are "unchanged"). (C) is the reverse of the author's claim that expense is *mistaken for* beauty. (D) overgeneralizes beyond the point being made.

### CARS-P04-Q4
- **Stem:** Why, according to the passage, does status-driven competition have no natural endpoint?
- **A:** Because the supply of goods is unlimited.
- **B:** Because people eventually satisfy all their material needs.
- **C:** Because status is relative, so each gain only resets the standard for the next comparison.
- **D:** Because governments continually raise the cost of living.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** The passage attributes the endlessness to "invidious comparison": "because status is relative, no absolute level of wealth is ever sufficient," and each gain "resets the standard." (A) and (D) are not mentioned. (B) contradicts the claim that the aim is position, not any fixed quantity of goods.

### CARS-P04-Q5
- **Stem:** A wealthy executive works long hours but ensures that his spouse never works and hosts lavish parties. In the passage's terms, the spouse's leisure and spending are best described as:
- **A:** conspicuous leisure performed by the executive himself.
- **B:** vicarious display that advertises the executive's status through another person.
- **C:** an example of invidious comparison ending the status competition.
- **D:** consumption motivated purely by the goods' usefulness.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage describes exactly this: display "delegated to others—to a spouse whose refinement and freedom from useful work advertise the earner's success," making the leisure and consumption "vicarious." (A) misassigns the display to the executive. (C) misuses "invidious comparison" and wrongly says competition ends. (D) contradicts the honorific (status) motive.

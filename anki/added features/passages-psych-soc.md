## SCI-PSY-P01
- **Discipline:** Psych/Soc
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage based on Radel R, Gruet M, Barzykowski K (2019), "Testing the ego-depletion effect in optimized conditions," *PLOS ONE* 14(3):e0213026, CC BY, https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0213026. No text reproduced verbatim; detail simplified.
- **Passage:** Many everyday failures—breaking a diet, snapping at a colleague, abandoning a hard task—are attributed to a lapse in self-control. One influential idea, the ego-depletion hypothesis, proposes that self-control draws on a limited internal resource, so that exerting it on one task leaves less available for the next. Early experiments seemed to support this, but the effect has become controversial: a large, pre-registered replication conducted across many laboratories found an effect statistically indistinguishable from zero, and meta-analyses suggested the earlier literature was inflated by publication bias, in which positive findings are more likely to be published than null ones.

  Researchers argued that some previous tests were poorly designed—using very short "depleting" tasks, loosely defined measures of self-control, and no baseline measurement—and set out to test the hypothesis under conditions optimized to reveal it if it exists. They reasoned that self-control depends chiefly on inhibitory control, the ability to suppress an automatic response, so they used conflict tasks that pit an automatic tendency against the task's instructions. The depleting manipulation was a Stroop task performed continuously for a full hour. In the high-inhibition condition, 75 percent of trials were incongruent (for example, the word "red" printed in blue ink, requiring participants to suppress the urge to read the word), whereas in the control condition none of the trials were incongruent, so little inhibition was required.

  Self-control was assessed with a separate Simon task, in which the location of a stimulus conflicts with the side of the correct response on incongruent trials. The key index was the interference effect—the performance difference between incongruent and congruent trials. Crucially, participants first completed the Simon task as a pre-test and were trained until their performance stabilized; this baseline meant that any change after the Stroop manipulation could be attributed to the manipulation rather than to pre-existing differences between individuals. If an hour of high-inhibition Stroop depletes self-control, the interference effect should grow from pre-test to post-test more in the high-inhibition condition than in the control.

  Two studies were run. Study 1 used a between-subjects design, assigning 82 participants to either the high-inhibition or the control condition. Study 2 used a within-subjects design with 52 participants, each of whom experienced both conditions on separate occasions. Study 1 produced a significant ego-depletion effect: after the demanding Stroop hour, participants showed a larger increase in interference and a greater decline in accuracy than controls. Study 2, however, did not reproduce this pattern. Because the two designs yielded conflicting outcomes even under conditions chosen to favor detection, the authors concluded that it remains difficult to be confident the ego-depletion effect is robust. The study illustrates why a single positive result is weak evidence: an effect that appears under one design or in one sample may fail to appear under another, and only consistent replication can distinguish a genuine phenomenon from noise.

### SCI-PSY-P01-Q1
- **Stem:** The "interference effect" on the Simon task—the difference in performance between incongruent and congruent trials—was used as a measure of:
- **A:** long-term memory capacity
- **B:** inhibitory (self-)control
- **C:** visual acuity
- **D:** verbal fluency
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage frames self-control as resting on inhibitory control and uses the conflict-driven interference effect as its index. (A), (C), and (D) name unrelated abilities the tasks were not designed to assess.

### SCI-PSY-P01-Q2
- **Stem:** Why did the researchers include a pre-test on the Simon task and train participants until performance stabilized?
- **A:** To deplete participants' self-control before the main task
- **B:** To establish a baseline so post-manipulation changes could be attributed to the Stroop manipulation rather than to pre-existing individual differences
- **C:** To ensure participants would fail the post-test
- **D:** To measure the Stroop effect directly
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage states the baseline meant "any change after the Stroop manipulation could be attributed to the manipulation rather than to pre-existing differences." (A) misidentifies the Simon pre-test as the depleting task (that was the Stroop). (C) is nonsensical as a design goal. (D) confuses the two tasks—the Stroop was the manipulation, the Simon the outcome measure.

### SCI-PSY-P01-Q3
- **Stem:** The high-inhibition and control conditions differed primarily in:
- **A:** the total duration of the Stroop task
- **B:** the proportion of incongruent trials (75% vs. 0%), i.e., the amount of inhibition required
- **C:** whether participants completed a Simon task at all
- **D:** the number of participants assigned to each
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** Both conditions ran the Stroop for the same hour; they differed in incongruent-trial proportion (75% vs. 0%), which sets the inhibition demand. (A) is wrong—duration was held constant. (C) is false—both did the Simon task. (D) is incidental and not the manipulated variable.

### SCI-PSY-P01-Q4
- **Stem:** Given that Study 1 (between-subjects) found a significant effect but Study 2 (within-subjects) did not, the most scientifically appropriate conclusion is that:
- **A:** the ego-depletion effect is definitively proven to be real
- **B:** the ego-depletion effect is definitively proven not to exist
- **C:** the evidence is mixed, so confidence in a robust effect is not warranted without further replication
- **D:** within-subjects designs are always invalid
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** Conflicting results across designs justify caution, not a firm verdict—exactly the authors' conclusion. (A) and (B) overclaim in opposite directions from mixed data. (D) is an unjustified blanket dismissal of a standard design.

### SCI-PSY-P01-Q5

- **A:** researchers to fabricate data
- **B:** journals and authors to publish positive/significant findings more readily than null findings
- **C:** participants to guess a study's hypothesis

## SCI-PSY-P02
- **Discipline:** Psych/Soc
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage based on Inglis M, O'Hagan S (2022), "Stereotype threat, gender and mathematics attainment: A conceptual replication of Stricker & Ward," *PLOS ONE* 17(5):e0267699, CC BY, https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0267699. No text reproduced verbatim; detail simplified.
- **Passage:** Stereotype threat refers to the apprehension people feel when they are at risk of confirming a negative stereotype about a group to which they belong. The worry itself is thought to consume attention and working memory, so that, for example, women who are reminded of a stereotype linking gender to mathematics ability may perform worse on a difficult math test than they otherwise would. A striking practical proposal grew out of this idea: if merely being reminded of one's group can depress performance, then the timing of a simple demographic question might matter. Asking test-takers to record their gender *after* completing a test, rather than *before* it, might remove the reminder at the moment it does the most harm.

  An earlier analysis of a large standardized examination suggested this was so, and estimated that moving the gender question to the end could translate into thousands of additional women each year earning credit on an advanced mathematics test. If correct, this would be an unusually cheap and simple intervention. Because claims that promise large effects from tiny changes deserve careful scrutiny, researchers conducted a pre-registered conceptual replication—publicly committing to their hypotheses, methods, and analysis plan in advance to guard against after-the-fact reinterpretation of the data, and testing the same underlying idea in a new setting rather than copying the original study exactly.

  The new setting was a national mathematics competition for younger students. Participants were randomly assigned to answer demographic questions, including gender, either before or after the mathematics problems, and their scores were compared. If the timing effect were real and general, students—particularly girls—who reported their gender after the test should outscore those who reported it beforehand.

  The result was a null one: the researchers found no evidence that the placement of the demographic questions affected performance. They concluded that relocating demographic questions on answer sheets is unlikely to be an effective remedy for gender gaps in mathematics. A failure to replicate of this kind does not by itself prove that stereotype threat never operates; effects can depend on the population, the difficulty of the test, and how strongly participants identify with the domain. What the study does show is that a specific, widely publicized intervention did not produce the promised benefit under a careful, pre-planned test, which weakens the case for adopting it as policy.

  The episode illustrates several principles of research. Conceptual replication tests whether a claimed effect generalizes beyond the exact conditions of the original study—which is what matters if the effect is to guide real decisions. Pre-registration protects against the temptation to search through many analyses until a significant result appears. And a large, precisely estimated null result is genuinely informative: rather than merely announcing that "nothing happened," it constrains how large any real effect could plausibly be, and cautions against building policy on interventions that sound compelling but have not withstood rigorous, transparent testing.

### SCI-PSY-P02-Q1
- **Stem:** What was the independent variable manipulated in the replication study?

- **B:** Whether demographic (gender) questions were placed before or after the test

- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** Participants were "randomly assigned to answer demographic questions... either before or after the mathematics problems," which is the manipulated variable. ## SCI-PSY-P03
- **Discipline:** Psych/Soc
- **Source:** Original synthetic passage based on Gajdos T, Régner I, Huguet P, Hainguerlot M, Vergnaud J-C, Sackur J, et al. (2019), "Does social context impact metacognition? Evidence from stereotype threat in a visual search task," *PLOS ONE* 14(4):e0215050, CC BY, https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0215050. No text reproduced verbatim; detail simplified.
- **Passage:** Metacognition is the monitoring of one's own mental processes—for instance, knowing how well one has performed, or how much confidence to place in a decision. Most research on stereotype threat, the apprehension of confirming a negative stereotype about one's group, has focused on how it impairs performance by taxing attention and working memory. Less is known about whether the same social situation changes metacognition. One hypothesis predicts that it should: if being under threat makes people more vigilant about their own behavior, they might actually become better at judging the quality of their decisions, even as the extra self-focus risks disrupting the task itself.

  To investigate, researchers had 125 university science students perform a visual search task. On each trial, participants searched a briefly presented array of letters for a target and reported a feature of it (its color). After each response they made two introspective judgments: how confident they were that their answer was correct, and how many items they felt they had scanned before finding the target. Roughly half of the participants (balanced across men and women) were placed under stereotype threat by framing the task as a measure of visuo-spatial or geometric ability—a domain carrying a negative stereotype about women—and telling them that previous studies had found a difference between men and women; the others were told that previous studies had found no such difference.

  From these judgments the researchers derived two measures of metacognitive monitoring. The first, based on confidence, was a calibration score capturing how well a participant's confidence matched the actual accuracy of their answers: good metacognition means being confident when correct and unsure when wrong. The second measure was more novel. To judge whether participants' sense of how many items they had scanned was accurate, the researchers needed an estimate of how many items were *actually* scanned, which cannot be observed directly. They obtained it from a simple computational model of visual search, then compared the model's estimate with each participant's reported number; a smaller gap indicated more accurate introspection about the search process itself.

  The results showed that stereotype threat *enhanced* metacognitive monitoring of both the outcome (confidence calibration) and the process (awareness of the search). In other words, participants under threat were, if anything, better at monitoring their own cognition than those who were not. The authors concluded that social context does not merely add noise to performance; it can reshape metacognition, plausibly by increasing the attention people direct toward their own internal states.

  The finding is a useful corrective to the assumption that stereotype threat produces only deficits. The same heightened self-monitoring that can interfere with a fluent, automatic task may sharpen a person's insight into their own decisions. Whether this heightened monitoring helps or hurts overall is likely to depend on the task: for skills that run best automatically, extra self-scrutiny may be costly, whereas for judgments that benefit from accurate self-assessment, it could be an advantage.

### SCI-PSY-P03-Q1
- **Stem:** As used in the passage, "metacognition" is best defined as:
- **A:** the speed at which a person can search a visual display
- **B:** the monitoring and evaluation of one's own cognitive processes, such as confidence in a decision
- **C:** the ability to memorize long lists of items
- **D:** an automatic physiological stress response
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** The passage defines metacognition as "the monitoring of one's own mental processes," including confidence judgments. (A) describes task performance, not metacognition. (C) describes memory capacity. (D) describes a stress response, which is a possible mechanism of threat but not the definition of metacognition.

### SCI-PSY-P03-Q5
- **Stem:** The study's central finding most directly challenges which assumption?
- **A:** That stereotype threat can only impair, and never enhance, aspects of cognition
- **B:** That confidence judgments can be measured

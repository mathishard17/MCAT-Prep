# MCAT Generated Cards — CARS (Social Sciences)

Self-contained CARS multiple-choice cards built from short **social-science**
passages (sociology, economics, anthropology, political theory, social
psychology, and history). Every passage is drawn from a **public-domain** work —
either an English original or a pre-1929 English translation — and is cited
inside the `Question` line (author, work, approximate year); each item is
answerable **from the passage alone**, with no outside knowledge required. Block
format matches `added features/generated-cards-cars.md` so the same line-based
importer can parse it.

CARS skill KCs (a prerequisite chain):

- `CARS::Foundations_of_Comprehension` — Prereqs: none
- `CARS::Reasoning_Within_the_Text` — Prereq: `Prereq::CARS::Foundations_of_Comprehension`
- `CARS::Reasoning_Beyond_the_Text` — Prereq: `Prereq::CARS::Reasoning_Within_the_Text`

Conventions:

- **Total: 21 cards — 7 per skill.**
- IDs: `MCAT-CARS-SOC-<TOPIC>-<NNN>`, unique per card (`<TOPIC>` = a short source tag).
- Section: `MCAT::CARS` for every card.
- The `Reasoning::` tag uses CARS-appropriate values — `Comprehension`,
  `Inference`, `Tone`, `Structure`, `Application`.
- `IRT::Guessing::0.25` for these four-choice items; `IRT::Discrimination::` in
  `0.7`–`1.5`.
- Skill mapping: Foundations = main idea / comprehension / vocabulary-in-context;
  Within = inference, tone, author's attitude, argument structure; Beyond =
  application to new situations, extension, evaluation of new evidence.
- Cited public-domain sources: Du Bois, Adam Smith, Veblen, Tocqueville, Sumner,
  Tylor, Cooley, Marx & Engels, Le Bon, Madison (*Federalist*), Paine, Douglass,
  Malthus, Addams, Follett, Frazer, Locke, Rousseau, Hobbes, Turner, Washington.

- **Question:** Passage (Rousseau, *The Social Contract*, ~1762): There is often a great deal of difference between the will of all and the general will; the latter considers only the common interest, while the former takes private interest into account, and is no more than a sum of particular wills. But take away from these same wills the pluses and minuses that cancel one another, and the general will remains as the sum of the differences. If, when the people held its deliberations, the citizens had no communication one with another, the grand total of the small differences would always give the general will. — Question: Which of the following situations best reflects the "general will" rather than the mere "will of all"?
- **A:** Each citizen votes for whichever policy most increases his own family's wealth.
- **B:** Citizens set aside their private advantage and choose the policy that serves the common interest.
- **C:** A powerful faction persuades a majority to vote for measures that enrich that faction.
- **D:** Every voter selects the option promising the largest personal payment to himself.
- **Correct:** B
- **Explanation:** Rousseau says the general will "considers only the common interest," whereas the will of all is a mere "sum of particular wills"; B captures the common-interest orientation, while A, C, and D are all aggregations of private or factional interest—precisely the will of all he contrasts it with.

- **Question:** Passage (Hobbes, *Leviathan*, ~1651): Hereby it is manifest, that during the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war; and such a war, as is of every man, against every man. In such condition there is no place for industry, because the fruit thereof is uncertain: and consequently no culture of the earth; no navigation; no commodious building; no account of time; no arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear, and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short. — Question: Based on the passage, the author would most likely argue that a society plagued by constant private violence needs which of the following?
- **A:** The complete removal of all authority so that people may live in natural freedom.
- **B:** A gradual weakening of central power in order to encourage individual industry.
- **C:** Acceptance that fear and scarcity are permanent and cannot be remedied at all.

- **Explanation:** Hobbes blames the "war of every man against every man" on living "without a common power to keep them all in awe," implying such a power is the remedy; A and B would deepen the very condition he deplores, and C ignores his point that the misery follows from the absence of authority rather than from any fixed decree of nature.

- **Difficulty:** 5
- **Question:** Passage (Turner, *The Frontier in American History*, ~1893): Up to our own day American history has been in a large degree the history of the colonization of the Great West. The existence of an area of free land, its continuous recession, and the advance of American settlement westward, explain American development. American social development has been continually beginning over again on the frontier. This perennial rebirth, this fluidity of American life, this expansion westward with its new opportunities, its continuous touch with the simplicity of primitive society, furnish the forces dominating American character. — Question: Which of the following findings, if true, would most seriously WEAKEN the author's thesis?
- **A:** The distinctive traits of American character emerged just as strongly in dense coastal cities untouched by frontier settlement.
- **B:** Successive waves of settlers repeatedly moved westward into newly opened areas of free land.
- **C:** Life on the frontier demanded self-reliance and improvisation from the settlers who lived it.

- **Correct:** A
- **Explanation:** Turner credits the frontier with shaping "American character," so evidence that the same traits arose where there was no frontier (dense coastal cities) directly undercuts the causal claim; B, C, and D are each consistent with—and would tend to support—his thesis rather than weaken it.

### MCAT-CARS-SOC-WASHINGTON-001

- **KC:** `CARS::Reasoning_Beyond_the_Text`
- **Prereqs:** `Prereq::CARS::Reasoning_Within_the_Text`
- **Difficulty:** 3
- **Question:** Passage (Washington, *Up from Slavery*, ~1901): To those of my race who depend on bettering their condition in a foreign land, or who underestimate the importance of cultivating friendly relations with the Southern white man, who is their next-door neighbor, I would say: "Cast down your bucket where you are"—cast it down in making friends in every manly way of the people of all races by whom we are surrounded. Cast it down in agriculture, in mechanics, in commerce, in domestic service, and in the professions. — Question: The advice to "cast down your bucket where you are" would most directly support which course of action?
- **A:** Emigrating to a distant country in search of wholly new opportunities.
- **B:** Waiting for outside benefactors to arrive before making any effort of one's own.
- **C:** Developing skills and relationships within one's present community and circumstances.
- **D:** Rejecting practical trades in favor of pursuits far removed from one's current situation.
- **Correct:** C
- **Explanation:** Washington urges people to "cast down your bucket where you are"—to build friendships and work "in agriculture, in mechanics, in commerce... and in the professions" right where they stand; C matches that local, self-directed effort, while A and D advise seeking opportunity elsewhere and B waits passively, all of which he explicitly counsels against.
